## 1. Malaria

Suggested slug: `malaria`

Overview: Malaria is a common infection in many parts of Nigeria and Africa. It can cause fever and body weakness, but not every fever is malaria. Testing and professional assessment matter because fever can also come from other infections or health problems.

Why it matters: Many families treat fever first at home or at a pharmacy. Delay, wrong assumptions, repeated unconfirmed treatment, or missed warning signs can create danger, especially for children, pregnant women, older adults, and people with other health conditions.

Common symptoms: fever, chills, headache, body aches, weakness, vomiting, poor appetite, sweating, general illness. Use wording that symptoms can overlap with other conditions.

Warning signs: repeated vomiting, confusion, convulsions, severe weakness, breathing difficulty, yellowing of the eyes, inability to drink, worsening fever, signs of dehydration, illness in a pregnant woman, serious illness in a baby or young child.

Hospital visit preparation: record when the fever started, temperature readings if available, medicines already taken, previous malaria tests, pregnancy status, child age/weight, any vomiting or convulsion, other symptoms such as cough, diarrhoea, or pain.

Questions to ask: What test or assessment is appropriate? Are there signs of severe illness? What should we watch for at home after professional care? When should we return urgently? What records should we keep?

Tests to understand: malaria rapid diagnostic test, blood film/microscopy, full blood count where clinically appropriate. Explain that a professional decides what tests fit the patient.

Records to keep: test result, prescription, dosage instructions as given by the professional, allergy history, visit notes, referral letter if referred.

Related centres: Infectious Disease / Fever Centre, Child Health Centre, Maternal Health Centre.

## 2. Hypertension

Suggested slug: `hypertension`

Overview: Hypertension means blood pressure remains higher than it should be over time. Many people may not feel symptoms, so regular measurement and records are important.

Why it matters: Poorly controlled blood pressure can increase risk for stroke, heart disease, kidney disease, and other complications. Many people only discover it during a crisis or routine check.

Common symptoms: often no symptoms; some people report headache, dizziness, chest discomfort, tiredness, or visual changes, but symptoms are not reliable for diagnosis.

Warning signs: chest pain, weakness on one side of the body, facial drooping, speech difficulty, severe headache with confusion, breathing difficulty, fainting, very high readings with symptoms. Urgent care is needed for alarming symptoms.

Hospital visit preparation: bring previous blood pressure readings, current medicines, herbal or over-the-counter medicines, previous test results, family history, diabetes/kidney records if any.

Questions to ask: How should I monitor blood pressure? What readings require urgent attention? What lifestyle steps matter? How should medicines be taken if prescribed? What follow-up schedule is appropriate?

Tests to understand: repeated blood pressure measurement, urine tests, kidney function tests, blood sugar, cholesterol, ECG where appropriate.

Records to keep: BP log, prescriptions, lab results, ECG reports, discharge summaries, referral letters.

Related centres: Heart & Blood Pressure Centre, Kidney Health, Stroke Warning Signs, Diabetes & Metabolic Health.

## 3. Diabetes

Suggested slug: `diabetes`

Overview: Diabetes is a condition where blood sugar regulation is abnormal. It requires professional diagnosis, follow-up, lifestyle education, and record continuity.

Why it matters: Diabetes can affect the eyes, kidneys, nerves, feet, heart, blood vessels, pregnancy outcomes, and infection risk. Many complications can be reduced with good follow-up and records.

Common symptoms: frequent urination, unusual thirst, weight change, tiredness, blurred vision, slow wound healing, recurrent infections. Some people have few symptoms.

Warning signs: confusion, severe weakness, vomiting, difficulty breathing, very high or very low sugar readings with symptoms, infected foot wound, pregnancy with high sugar concerns.

Hospital visit preparation: bring blood sugar readings, prescriptions, previous lab tests, eye/foot/kidney results, diet questions, current symptoms, pregnancy status where relevant.

Questions to ask: What type of diabetes is suspected or confirmed? What targets should I monitor? What complications should be checked? How often should I follow up? What records should I keep?

Tests to understand: fasting/random blood glucose, HbA1c, urine tests, kidney function, cholesterol, eye checks, foot checks.

Records to keep: blood sugar logs, HbA1c results, prescriptions, kidney tests, eye reports, foot assessment notes, discharge summaries.

Related centres: Diabetes & Metabolic Health Centre, Kidney Disease Awareness, Heart & Blood Pressure Centre.

## 4. Asthma

Suggested slug: `asthma`

Overview: Asthma is a long-term breathing condition that can cause episodes of wheezing, cough, chest tightness, and breathing difficulty. It needs professional assessment and a clear action plan.

Why it matters: Some attacks can become serious. Many people confuse repeated cough or breathing difficulty with ordinary infection, dust reaction, or weakness.

Common symptoms: wheezing, cough especially at night or early morning, chest tightness, breathlessness, symptoms triggered by dust, smoke, exercise, cold air, infections, or allergens.

Warning signs: severe breathing difficulty, difficulty speaking, blue lips, drowsiness, chest pulling in children, symptoms not improving as expected, repeated attacks.

Hospital visit preparation: record triggers, frequency of symptoms, night symptoms, medicines/inhalers used, previous hospital visits, allergies, family history, school/work exposure.

Questions to ask: Is this asthma or another breathing problem? How should inhalers be used if prescribed? What are danger signs? What triggers should be avoided? Is a written action plan needed?

Tests to understand: lung function tests/spirometry where available, oxygen level, chest assessment, chest x-ray only where professionally indicated.

Records to keep: inhaler names, prescription, action plan, admission/discharge notes, trigger history, school/work letters if relevant.

Related centres: Respiratory Health Centre, Pneumonia Guide, Child Health Centre.

## 5. Typhoid fever

Suggested slug: `typhoid-fever`

Overview: Typhoid fever is an infection usually linked with contaminated food or water. Symptoms can overlap with malaria and other illnesses, so professional assessment matters.

Why it matters: In many communities, typhoid is assumed without proper confirmation. Repeated self-treatment or poor testing interpretation can delay correct care.

Common symptoms: prolonged fever, headache, weakness, abdominal pain, diarrhoea or constipation, poor appetite, body aches.

Warning signs: persistent high fever, severe abdominal pain, confusion, repeated vomiting, dehydration, blood in stool, worsening weakness.

Hospital visit preparation: write fever duration, stool changes, vomiting, food/water exposure, medicines taken, previous tests, malaria results, travel or outbreak exposure.

Questions to ask: What tests are reliable in this situation? Could this be another cause of fever? What warning signs require urgent return? How can household prevention be improved?

Tests to understand: blood culture where available and appropriate, full blood count, stool testing where indicated, clinician interpretation of Widal and other tests.

Records to keep: test results, prescriptions, hydration advice given by clinician, referral/discharge notes.

Related centres: Infectious Disease / Fever Centre, Nigerian Health Context, Access Infrastructure Desk.

## 6. Pneumonia

Suggested slug: `pneumonia`

Overview: Pneumonia is an infection or inflammation affecting the lungs. It can be serious, especially in children, older adults, pregnant women, and people with chronic illness.

Why it matters: Cough and fever can look ordinary, but breathing difficulty, chest pain, or fast breathing can signal a more serious problem.

Common symptoms: cough, fever, chest pain, fast breathing, weakness, poor feeding in children, chills, breathing discomfort.

Warning signs: difficulty breathing, chest indrawing in children, blue lips, confusion, inability to feed or drink, severe chest pain, persistent high fever, worsening illness.

Hospital visit preparation: record cough duration, fever timeline, breathing changes, previous medicines, vaccination history, child age, other illnesses, oxygen readings if available.

Questions to ask: Are there signs of serious lung infection? What follow-up is needed? What danger signs should we watch? Are there prevention steps such as vaccination to discuss?

Tests to understand: chest examination, oxygen saturation, chest x-ray where indicated, blood tests where appropriate.

Records to keep: prescription, x-ray report, discharge notes, vaccination record, referral letter.

Related centres: Respiratory Health Centre, Child Health Centre, Infectious Disease / Fever Centre.

## 7. Peptic ulcer disease / ulcer awareness

Suggested slug: `peptic-ulcer-disease`

Overview: Peptic ulcer disease involves sores in the stomach or upper intestine lining. Many people call many kinds of abdominal pain “ulcer,” but proper assessment is important.

Why it matters: Some abdominal symptoms may come from indigestion, medication effects, infection, gallbladder problems, pregnancy-related issues, or emergencies. Dangerous symptoms should not be ignored.

Common symptoms: burning upper abdominal pain, bloating, nausea, discomfort related to meals. Symptoms vary.

Warning signs: vomiting blood, black stool, severe sudden abdominal pain, fainting, persistent vomiting, weight loss, difficulty swallowing, severe weakness.

Hospital visit preparation: note pain location, timing with meals, medicines such as painkillers, alcohol/smoking history, previous tests, pregnancy status, weight loss, bleeding symptoms.

Questions to ask: What could be causing this pain? Are any warning signs present? What tests are appropriate? How should pain medicines be used safely?

Tests to understand: clinician assessment, H. pylori testing where indicated, stool blood test, endoscopy where appropriate and available.

Records to keep: prescriptions, test results, endoscopy reports, referral letters, discharge summaries.

Related centres: Digestive Health Centre if existing, Health Library, Access Infrastructure Desk.

## 8. Pregnancy and antenatal care

Suggested slug: `pregnancy-antenatal-care`

Overview: Antenatal care is structured care during pregnancy to support the health of the pregnant woman and baby. It involves scheduled visits, monitoring, tests, education, and birth planning.

Why it matters: Pregnancy can be normal and still require regular monitoring. Early recognition of warning signs and good record-keeping can improve care continuity.

Common topics: antenatal booking, blood pressure checks, urine tests, blood tests, fetal growth monitoring, nutrition education, birth planning, danger signs.

Warning signs: bleeding, severe abdominal pain, severe headache, blurred vision, swelling with symptoms, convulsions, reduced fetal movement later in pregnancy, fever, severe vomiting, breathlessness.

Hospital visit preparation: carry antenatal card, scan reports, lab tests, prescriptions, blood group result, previous pregnancy history, allergies, emergency contact.

Questions to ask: What is my pregnancy risk status? What tests are due? What warning signs need urgent care? Where should delivery be planned? What records should I keep?

Tests to understand: blood pressure, urine protein/sugar, blood group, genotype where relevant, HIV/hepatitis/syphilis tests as guided by professionals, ultrasound where indicated.

Records to keep: antenatal card, scan reports, lab results, prescriptions, referral letters, discharge notes.

Related centres: Maternal Health Centre, Patient Rights, Nigerian Health Context, Access Infrastructure Desk.

## 9. Child fever

Suggested slug: `child-fever`

Overview: Fever in a child is common, but the cause can vary. Families should understand how to observe, record, and seek professional care when needed.

Why it matters: Children can worsen quickly. Age, feeding, hydration, breathing, convulsions, and behaviour matter when deciding urgency.

Common symptoms: raised temperature, poor appetite, tiredness, crying, vomiting, cough, diarrhoea, body warmth, reduced play.

Warning signs: child younger than three months with fever, convulsions, difficulty breathing, inability to drink or breastfeed, repeated vomiting, drowsiness, stiff neck, dehydration, persistent high fever, rash with serious illness.

Hospital visit preparation: record age, weight if known, temperature readings, fever duration, medicines already given, feeding, urine/stool changes, immunization history, malaria test if done.

Questions to ask: What signs show the child is seriously ill? What should we monitor at home? When should we return urgently? What records should we keep?

Tests to understand: malaria test, full blood count, urine test, other tests depending on professional assessment.

Records to keep: immunization card, prescriptions, test results, discharge notes, referral letters.

Related centres: Child Health Centre, Malaria, Pneumonia, Access Infrastructure Desk.

## 10. Stroke warning signs

Suggested slug: `stroke-warning-signs`

Overview: Stroke happens when blood flow to part of the brain is blocked or bleeding occurs in the brain. It is a medical emergency. Public education should focus on recognition and urgent hospital care.

Why it matters: Delay can worsen outcomes. Families may first assume spiritual attack, weakness, stress, or ordinary collapse. Recognition matters.

Warning signs: face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty, sudden confusion, sudden severe headache, sudden vision problem, sudden balance difficulty, one-sided weakness or numbness.

Urgent care: Make clear that suspected stroke requires immediate emergency medical attention at an appropriate hospital.

Hospital preparation: note exact time symptoms started or when the person was last seen normal, current medicines, blood pressure/diabetes history, previous stroke, allergies, patient ID, emergency contact.

Questions to ask professionals: What type of stroke is suspected? What tests are needed urgently? What follow-up and rehabilitation may be needed? What risk factors should be managed after emergency care?

Tests to understand: brain scan where available, blood pressure, blood sugar, blood tests, ECG where appropriate.

Records to keep: scan report, discharge summary, medication list, referral notes, rehabilitation plan, BP/sugar logs.

Related centres: Heart & Blood Pressure Centre, Hypertension, Diabetes, Patient Rights, Access Infrastructure Desk.

## 11. Kidney disease awareness

Suggested slug: `kidney-disease-awareness`

Overview: Kidney disease means the kidneys are not filtering the blood or balancing body fluids and minerals as well as they should. It may develop silently.

Why it matters: Hypertension, diabetes, infections, some medicines, and other conditions can affect kidney health. Early professional follow-up and records are important.

Common symptoms: swelling, tiredness, changes in urination, high blood pressure, nausea, itching, breathlessness, but symptoms may appear late.

Warning signs: severe swelling, breathlessness, very reduced urine, confusion, severe weakness, uncontrolled blood pressure, abnormal test results requiring urgent professional review.

Hospital preparation: bring BP records, diabetes records, past kidney tests, urine tests, medicine list including painkillers/herbal products, swelling timeline.

Questions to ask: What do the kidney test results mean? What conditions may be affecting the kidneys? How often should monitoring occur? What medicines should be used carefully?

Tests to understand: creatinine/eGFR, urine protein, electrolytes, ultrasound where indicated, blood pressure and diabetes monitoring.

Records to keep: kidney function tests, urine tests, ultrasound reports, prescriptions, BP/sugar logs, referral notes.

Related centres: Diabetes & Metabolic Health, Heart & Blood Pressure, Hypertension, Access Infrastructure Desk.

## 12. Mental health distress awareness

Suggested slug: `mental-health-distress-awareness`

Overview: Mental health distress can include deep sadness, anxiety, fear, confusion, trauma reactions, substance-related problems, or changes in behaviour. It deserves compassionate professional attention.

Why it matters: Stigma, silence, family misunderstanding, and delayed care can make distress worse. People need respectful pathways to help.

Common signs: persistent sadness, severe worry, withdrawal, sleep changes, appetite changes, loss of interest, panic feelings, unusual behaviour, difficulty functioning, substance misuse concerns.

Urgent warning signs: immediate danger to self or others, severe confusion, violent behaviour, inability to care for oneself, psychosis-like symptoms, severe substance intoxication or withdrawal. Use safe wording and direct urgent professional/emergency help without describing self-harm methods.

Hospital/clinic preparation: describe behaviour changes, duration, triggers, medicines/substances used, sleep/appetite changes, previous episodes, family concerns, safety concerns.

Questions to ask: What kind of professional support is appropriate? What warning signs require urgent care? How can family support without shame? What follow-up plan is needed?

Tests to understand: physical health review, medication/substance history, mental health assessment. Avoid overclaiming.

Records to keep: appointment notes, prescriptions, referral letters, discharge summaries, safety/follow-up plan from professionals.

Related centres: Mental Health Centre, Community Health Literacy, Patient Rights, Nigerian Health Context.
